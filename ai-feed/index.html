California's Poppy AI - State Workers Build and Deploy Claude Across 67 Departments

California deployed Poppy, an AI assistant built internally by state workers using Claude, to more than 2,800 employees across 67 state departments, with full statewide rollout on track for July 2026. Unlike off-the-shelf government chatbots, Poppy ships with pre-built query templates tuned to common state workflows - permit processing, constituent correspondence, budget analysis - rather than open-ended chat, making it one of the most structured public-sector AI deployments in the US. The in-house build model signals a new procurement pattern: agencies buy underlying model access and own the application layer, keeping sensitive workflow logic and audit trails under public-sector control.

What's moving
  • State-built on top of a vendor model rewrites the government AI procurement conversation - instead of buying a full AI product and inheriting the vendor's terms, agencies can procure model access and own the application layer, keeping policy templates and compliance logic inside the public sector
  • Pre-built query templates are the key design choice: they constrain what Poppy answers and how, reducing hallucination risk for high-stakes administrative outputs like permits, benefits determinations, and legal correspondence, while making outputs auditable in ways a raw chatbot interface is not
  • If the July statewide rollout succeeds at California's scale, Poppy becomes a reference architecture every other US state and federal agency can benchmark against - positioning the Claude-based, state-built model as the de facto template for responsible public-sector AI deployment

2026 Is the Year AI Leaves the Cloud - On-Device Models Move From Demo to Default

2026 is shaping up as the inflection year when capable AI moves from cloud-dependent to locally executed on smartphones, IoT sensors, and industrial edge hardware - without constant connectivity. Advances in model compression, sparse activation architectures, and dedicated neural processing chips have reduced the compute threshold for capable inference below what a premium mobile SoC can sustain continuously. The shift unlocks three previously gated categories simultaneously: real-time latency-sensitive applications like robotics and live translation, privacy-by-default compliance for healthcare and finance, and connectivity-independent access for billions of users in emerging markets.

What's moving
  • On-device inference eliminates the datacenter round-trip that makes real-time AI feel sluggish for latency-critical applications - robotics, vehicle systems, medical devices, and live translation all need sub-5ms response that cloud inference cannot reliably match, opening application categories that cloud AI structurally cannot enter
  • Privacy is the enterprise procurement unlock: data processed on-device never leaves the organization's perimeter, making on-device AI compliant by default for healthcare, legal, and financial workflows that cloud AI currently requires contractual overlays and data processing agreements to address
  • Emerging-market adoption is the largest unpriced opportunity - billions of users with limited or expensive data connectivity become full AI users the moment inference runs locally, and companies that establish on-device model presence in those markets before cloud costs fall will hold a structural first-mover advantage
