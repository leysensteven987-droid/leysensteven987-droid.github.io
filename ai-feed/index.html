Anthropic Discovers a Hidden Global Workspace Inside Claude That Mirrors Human Conscious Reasoning

Anthropic's new "J-lens" interpretability tool has revealed a small internal workspace - J-Space - inside Claude where all deliberate reasoning takes place. When researchers suppressed J-Space, shallow tasks like factual recall survived intact while multi-hop inference, analogy completion, translation, and creative writing collapsed to below-chance levels. The structure closely mirrors the Global Workspace Theory of consciousness, where a central bottleneck broadcasts information to specialized cognitive systems. Anthropic stops short of claiming sentience but the structural parallel is precise enough to sharpen the debate over what is actually happening inside frontier models.

What's moving
  • The J-lens is the first interpretability tool that distinguishes computations Claude "attends to" from the larger ocean of automatic processing beneath; J-Space handles all tasks requiring flexible reasoning while representing only a small fraction of total compute - a two-tier split that maps directly onto the system 1 / system 2 distinction in cognitive science
  • Suppression experiments showed factual recall and grammar survived essentially intact while higher-order tasks collapsed completely, establishing J-Space as a specific architectural feature that deliberate reasoning routes through rather than a general compute bottleneck that all processing shares equally
  • Gizmodo and rival interpretability researchers warned the paper should not be read uncritically - J-Space may be detecting computational load rather than a consciousness-adjacent structure, and Anthropic's Global Workspace framing is one of several interpretations; the result has already seeded a live debate in the interpretability community over what bottleneck structures inside LLMs actually mean
