Kimi K3 - Moonshot AI Ships the Largest Open-Source Model Ever and Debuts at Number 3 Globally

China's Moonshot AI released Kimi K3 on July 16, a 2.8-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model with a 1-million-token context window and 896 experts of which only 16 activate per token - the largest open-source model ever shipped, with inference costs far below what the raw parameter count implies. K3 debuted at number 3 on the Artificial Analysis AI leaderboard, behind only Claude Fable 5 and GPT-5.6 Sol, and outperformed both on Arena.ai's front-end web development benchmark. Full open weights are promised by July 27, putting near-frontier performance within reach of any well-resourced organization outside the major cloud providers.

What's moving
  • A Chinese lab reaching the top three globally with a fully open-weight release is a geopolitical signal as much as a technical one - the model closes the frontier capability gap in a way US chip export controls cannot easily reverse, since architectural knowledge travels with the weights rather than the hardware used to train them
  • Full 2.8T open weights by July 27 make fine-tuning and self-hosting at near-frontier quality tractable for the first time for well-resourced organizations; combined with K3's sparse MoE profile, the inference cost is far lower than the headline parameter count implies, making on-premise deployment genuinely competitive
  • Kimi K3 pricing at $3/$15 per million tokens matches Anthropic's Sonnet tier exactly - the first time a Chinese lab has entered at premium Western pricing rather than discounting for share, which reframes the competitive dynamic from "cheaper Chinese alternative" to "quality parity at the same price"

SK Hynix Lists on Nasdaq as SKHY in $28B IPO - Public Markets Get Their First Direct Bet on AI Memory

SK Hynix, which alongside Samsung controls more than 95% of the world's high-bandwidth memory (HBM) supply, listed on the Nasdaq as SKHY on July 10, raising $28 billion in what was the second-largest share sale in market history and was seven times oversubscribed. HBM is the memory architecture that makes large-scale AI training and inference economically possible, and until now there was no liquid public instrument for investors to bet directly on AI infrastructure demand at the chip level. SKHY changes that, creating a real-time market barometer for the one input that every frontier AI lab cannot build without.

What's moving
  • Seven-times oversubscription on a $28 billion offering signals the market views HBM as a strategic chokepoint rather than a commodity - one that SK Hynix and Samsung structurally control, and which no new entrant can credibly replicate within a meaningful planning horizon given the capital and process complexity of HBM fabrication
  • IPO proceeds go directly to advanced fab construction and EUV lithography equipment for next-generation HBM4 production - the offering is effectively public markets financing the AI industry's 2027-2028 compute expansion, transferring a significant portion of that capex risk from corporate balance sheets to investors
  • SKHY now creates a live correlation between AI demand signals and a publicly traded equity - when a frontier lab announces a large training run or a cloud hyperscaler adjusts AI capex guidance, HBM demand is the most direct downstream indicator, and that now prices into a tradeable instrument in real time

1,600 AI Agents Per Enterprise by Year-End - and 70% of Companies Cannot Govern What They Already Have

IBM projects the average large enterprise will be running 1,600 AI agents by the end of 2026, but 70% of those same organizations say they cannot govern the agents they currently operate, and only 18% maintain an accurate real-time inventory of what is running where. Gartner forecasts more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027, driven by cost overruns and insufficient risk controls - despite agents that do reach production averaging 171% ROI. The gap between deployment speed and governance readiness is now the defining operational risk in enterprise AI.

What's moving
  • Agent sprawl across business units without centralized oversight is the enterprise equivalent of shadow IT - but unlike a rogue spreadsheet, a misconfigured agent holds execution authority over live systems and can initiate transactions, send external communications, and modify records before any audit trail catches up
  • The 171% average ROI for agents that reach production explains why enterprises push ahead despite governance failures: the payoff is large enough that organizations absorb the risk of getting it wrong, creating a perverse incentive where the strongest business case for deploying fast is also the strongest case for cutting governance corners
  • Gartner's 40% cancellation forecast maps closely onto the highest-complexity, lowest-auditability deployments - document processing, compliance checks, and invoice handling are the early survivors because their outputs are measurable; open-ended reasoning agents with wide execution authority are the ones most likely to be cut first
